Comparison of Mineral Oil, Natural Ester Oil, and Synthetic Ester Oil as Transformer Insulation FluidsExecutive SummaryMineral oil has long been the standard transformer insulating fluid due to its low cost and good dielectric/cooling performance. However, it presents safety and environmental drawbacks – it is flammable (flash point ~140 °C) and non-biodegradable, posing fire and pollution risks. In contrast, natural ester (vegetable-based) and synthetic ester (chemically engineered) fluids offer significant improvements in fire safety and environmental sustainability. Both ester types are far less flammable (fire points >300 °C) and readily biodegradable. Natural esters are derived from renewable plant oils, making thembiodegradable and non-toxic, and they excel at preserving transformer paperinsulation by absorbing moisture. Synthetic esters share the fire-safe andbiodegradable nature and are formulated for greater oxidative stability under stress.In summary, mineral oil is economical but flammable and environmentally hazardous, natural esters are eco-friendly and fire-safe but require sealed systems (oxidation control) and come at higher cost, and synthetic esters combine fire safety andoxidation stability for demanding applications, albeit at the highest initial cost. Mineral oil has decent thermal stability in normal operation, but it islimited to about 110 °C hottest spot for long-term life of cellulose insulation. At elevated temperatures, mineral oil oxidizes and breaks down, forming acids and sludge that can impair cooling and insulation. In free-breathing transformers(exposed to air), inhibitors are added to mineral oil to slow oxidation, but over timethe oil’s dielectric strength and color deteriorate, and sludge can precipitate. Thus, mineral oil-filled units are typically designed not to exceed standardtemperature rise limits (65 °C average winding rise) to avoid accelerating oil aging.Natural ester oils exhibit higher thermal resilience. They can safely operate at higher temperatures – industry practice and standards (e.g. IEEE C57.154) allow up to~130 °C hotspot when using natural ester with thermally upgraded paper, about20 °C higher than mineral oil limits. This is because natural esters degrademore slowly at a given high temperature, and crucially, they protect solid insulation:the fluid’s high moisture affinity draws water out of paper, significantly slowing paperthermal aging. In essence, transformers filled with natural ester can endurehigher loads/temperatures for a given lifespan. Natural-ester-filled transformers are usually sealed or equipped with nitrogen blankets or desiccant breathers.With such measures, natural esters have performed well, but onemust monitor acid number and dielectric tan-delta as the fluid ages. If properly maintained (or in sealed units), natural esters do not produce the sludgedeposits that mineral oil can – their degradation primarily results in a gradual increasein acid and viscosity rather than sudden sludge precipitation.Synthetic ester oils are engineered for superior thermal and oxidative stability.Composed of synthesized fatty acid esters (often pentaerythritol tetracarboxylates or similar), these fluids have saturated bonds that resist oxidation much better than theunsaturated chains in natural esters. As a result, synthetic esters can beused in free-breathing transformers and harsh conditions with less concern foroxidation. They typically require little or no antioxidant additive. Over long periods, synthetic esters will eventually oxidize too, but at a slower rate; they tend to form far less sludge. In accelerated aging tests, synthetic esters maintain low acidity for a longer time than natural esters, only rising significantly after prolonged high-temperature exposure (e.g. thousands of hours). This makes them well-suited for high-load or high-temperature applications (such as wind farmtransformers, industrial or traction transformers) where fluid replacement intervalsneed to be long. In terms of pure thermal endurance, natural esters can actually haveslightly higher fire/boiling thresholds (due to heavier molecular structure) ,but synthetic esters’ key advantage is oxidative stability – they remain stable in air at high temperatures that would rapidly age a natural ester. Thus, both ester typesoutperform mineral oil in thermal stability (esters can handle higher temps with lessdegradation), with synthetic esters providing the most robustness forprolonged high-temperature operation.Biodegradability and Environmental ImpactEnvironmental considerations are a major factor in fluid selection today. Mineral oil is petroleum-derived and not readily biodegradable. If spilled into soil or water, it tends to persist; natural breakdown is very slow (mineral oils are considered “ultimatelynon-biodegradable” in practical terms). A mineral oil spill can contaminategroundwater or streams and is toxic to aquatic organisms. Utilities mustimplement costly spill containment systems and have remediation plans for mineral oil leaks. The fluid is also classified as hazardous waste in many jurisdictions whendisposed due to its poor biodegradability and possible presence of toxic additives orPCB contaminants in older transformers.Natural esters are vastly superior in this regard. Being made from vegetable oils (e.g. soy, rapeseed), they are readily biodegradable – typically over 95–99% biodegradedin standard 28-day tests. In fact, natural esters are often termed “ultimatelybiodegradable” because they can break down completely into natural substances(CO₂, water, biomass) given a little time and microbial action. For example, tests perOECD 301B show ~97% biodegradation for certain natural ester fluids. Thismeans if a natural ester leak occurs, the environmental damage is minimal and short- term, especially compared to a mineral oil spill. Natural esters are also non-toxic to flora and fauna – they are derived from food-grade seed oils in many cases, without heavy metals or halogens. Their renewable origin further means they have a much lower carbon footprint over their life cycle. Using plant-based oil sequesters CO₂(from the growing plants) and can make the fluid effectively carbon neutral.These traits make natural ester fluids very attractive for transformers in sensitive areas (near waterways, protected ecosystems, or urban/substation sites where oil spills must be avoided). Regulators and insurers often favor biodegradable fluids, which canreduce penalties or containment requirements.Synthetic esters are also readily biodegradable (usually >90% in 28-daybiodegradation tests). Although synthesized, they are organic compoundsthat microbes can ultimately decompose. Synthetic ester fluids are free of PCBs,silicones, or other persistent toxins, so they do not linger as long-term pollutants.The life-cycle carbon footprint of synthetic ester can be similar to or somewhat lower than mineral oil, but not as low as a natural ester which is derived from atmospheric carbon via plants. In terms of ecotoxicity, synthetic esters are regarded as non-toxic. Both natural and synthetic esters are classified as environmentally acceptable dielectric fluids. Overall, ester fluids greatly reduce environmental risk: for instance, utilities have reported that using esters avoids expensive oil containment systems, and any accidental leak requires far less remediation effort.Cost ConsiderationsCost is often a deciding factor in fluid choice, balancing initial expense against operational benefits. Mineral oil is the cheapest option by a wide margin. It is mass- produced by refining crude oil, resulting in a low per-liter price (on the order of a fewUSD per liter, though prices fluctuate with oil markets). Because mineral oilhas been the industry standard, transformers are optimized for it and supply chainsare well-established. Thus, upfront equipment costs with mineral oil are minimal – it’sone reason mineral oil has dominated for decades.Both types of ester fluids are more expensive upfront. Natural esters (e.g. bioTRANSOL of Savita, etc.) typically cost significantly more per volumethan mineral oil, partly due to the cost of vegetable oil feedstock and the processing (esterification, purification) required to meet dielectric specs. In the U.S., natural ester fluids have been cited at roughly twice the price per gallon of mineral oil (and evenhigher in regions where import or production costs apply). This translatesto a higher purchase price for ester-filled transformers. Manufacturers estimate new distribution transformers filled with natural ester incur about a 10-15% higher initialcost compared to equivalent mineral-oil units. Large power transformersmight see a slightly higher premium due to larger volumes of fluid. Despite this, the life-cycle cost of natural ester can be favorable – its ability to extendinsulation life and reduce fire safety infrastructure can offset the upfront premium. For example, using bioTRANSOL fluid can prolong transformer life (byslowing paper aging), yielding savings that outweigh the initial fluid cost over time.Additionally, the reduced risk of fire and environmental incidentscan lower insurance and mitigation costs. Some utilities report that, considering these factors, natural ester units are cost-effective in the long run, especially forenvironmentally sensitive installations.Synthetic esters are generally the most expensive insulating fluid. They aremanufactured via chemical processes (often from polyol alcohols and fatty acids),which can be costly. Synthetic esters are not produced in the same volume as mineral oil, also contributing to higher cost. Estimates vary, but a synthetic ester like Transol Synth 100 can cost on the order of 5–6 times the price of mineral oil per liter in somemarkets. In transformer procurement, using synthetic ester might raise theequipment cost by roughly 25–30% versus a mineral oil unit. This premium is usually justifiable only when the performance or risk reduction is critical – for instance, in a transformer located indoors or offshore where fire risk and maintenance access is paramount. In those cases, the cost of an ester fluid is small compared to the potential cost of a fire or environmental cleanup. It’s worth notingthat as ester fluids become more common, economies of scale may gradually reduce costs. Already, some utilities & industries in purchase only ester-filled power and distribution transformers, finding the total ownership cost favorable after accounting for longer life and avoided spill infrastructure. In summary, mineral oil wins on upfront cost, but esters (natural and synthetic) can provide better value over the transformer’s lifespan through risk mitigation, extended life, and regulatory compliance. Each organization must evaluate this trade-off; many find the safety and longevity benefits of esters justify the higher initial investment.Fire SafetyA critical advantage of ester fluids is their superior fire safety. Mineral oil is acombustible liquid with a relatively low flash point around 140 °C and fire pointaround 160 °C. In practice this means if a transformer tank ruptures oran electrical arc occurs, mineral oil can ignite readily from heat or sparks. Onceignited, it burns vigorously; a large mineral oil spill fire can be catastrophic, spreading to equipment and structures. There have been numerous substation fires attributed to mineral oil, and they often require extensive fire suppression efforts. Consequently,installations using mineral oil must incorporate fire mitigation: e.g. fire walls between transformers, deluge sprinkler or foam systems, separation distances, andcontainment pits to catch burning oil. These measures add cost and complexity butare necessary because a mineral-oil-fed fire can self-sustain until the fuel is exhausted. The energy content of mineral oil is high (around 42 MJ/kg), meaning it releases a lot of heat if it burns. It also can produce toxic smoke when burning (containing soot and various hydrocarbons).Natural ester fluids have much higher ignition thresholds. Typically, a natural ester’sflash point is in the 300–330 °C range and fire point ~350–360 °C. These values are more than double those of mineral oil, qualifying natural esters as K-class fluids (less flammable). In a controlled arc test, a transformer filled with a K-class fluid will often self-extinguish: the fluid may carbonize locally but does not continueburning once the arc is quenched. In fact, tests have shown aninternal arc in a natural-ester-filled transformer did not ignite the fluid at all. Even if the ester is sprayed (e.g. due to a ruptured tank) and an externalflame source is present, it will burn only as long as that ignition source is applied andtends to self-extinguish when external heat is removed. This dramaticallyreduces fire risk. Natural esters are FM Global approved and UL classified as less-flammable transformer liquids. The practical benefit is that transformers with natural ester can often be placed in locations not feasible for mineral oil units – for example, inside buildings, on rooftops, offshore platforms, or in densely populated areas – with minimal fire mitigation. Fire walls and moat-like basins might be eliminated orreduced in size. Many wind farm and solar farm transformersnow use natural esters to minimize fire hazards in remote or sensitive sites. The high fire point also means utilities can handle overloads without as much fear of a transformer fire. Overall, natural esters virtually eliminate the chance of a pool firefrom a transformer, improving safety for personnel and infrastructure.Synthetic esters are similarly fire-safe. Most synthetic esters have flash points above~265 °C and fire points ~315 °C, squarely in the K-class category. Theyhave slightly lower ignition points than natural esters on average, but stillfar above mineral oil. In real-world terms, a synthetic ester-filled unit has an excellent fire safety record – for instance, offshore wind substations have used synthetic ester transformers specifically to mitigate fire, and have reported zero fire incidents, withdesigns even claiming a “100% fire safety record” for ester-filled transformers. Like natural esters, synthetic esters do not sustain combustion easily. If a tank rupture occurs, the fluid’s high fire point and lower heat of combustion meanany fire is limited in scope and tends to self-extinguish when the initial energy (froman arc or external flame) dissipates. For this reason, syntheticesters are used in indoor substations and in transformers located in sensitive orconfined spaces (e.g. inside wind turbine nacelles or in tunnels). Similar to natural ester, using synthetic fluids can simplify fire protection systems – often no need forcostly automatic fire suppressants or expansive containment moats. Additionally, insurance companies often look favorably on less-flammable fluids, potentially lowering insurance premiums for facilities. In summary, both natural and synthetic esters vastly improve fire safety relative to mineral oil.They essentially eliminate the pool fire scenario and allow safer transformeroperation, whereas mineral oil requires careful risk management to avoid catastrophic fires.Performance Under Load“Performance under load” encompasses how the fluid supports transformer operation during normal and peak loading – including cooling efficiency, dielectric behavior under electrical stress, and the fluid’s interaction with the insulating paper as loadvaries.Mineral oil has excellent viscosity characteristics for cooling: it is relatively thin atoperating temperatures (e.g. ~10 cSt at 40 °C, ~3 cSt at 100 °C). This meansit circulates easily through radiator fins and windings, carrying heat away efficiently.Its thermal conductivity and specific heat are decent (on the order of 0.12 W/m·K and1.8 J/g·K at 20 °C), which help in heat transfer. Thus, mineral oil has beeneffective in keeping transformers cool under load. Its dielectric strength is high(typically >50 kV breakdown voltage in a 2.5 mm gap when dry), so it insulates well under electrical stress. Under moderate loads (within design), mineral oil maintains stable performance. However, under heavy or prolonged overloads, some limitations emerge. First, high hot-spot temperatures accelerate mineral oil’s degradation(creating sludge that can clog cooling ducts). Second, and critically, mineral oil’s low water saturation point means that even a small increase in moisture (from paperaging or leaks) can lead to free water or significantly reduced dielectric strength. At 20–25 °C, mineral oil can hold only around 50–60 ppm of water before it’s saturated. Under load, as the paper releases moisture (aging produces water), mineral oil cannot absorb much of it, so the moisture stays in the paper insulation, which further reduces paper dielectric strength and accelerates its breakdown. This creates a vicious cycle under high load: heat ages the paper, paper releases water, mineral oil can’t absorb it, paper gets wetter and ages faster.Therefore, running mineral-oil transformers near or beyond nameplate capacity forlong periods significantly shortens insulation life. Operators typically avoid continuous overloading of mineral-oil units or use cooling upgrades (fans, pumps) to keeptemperatures in check.Natural ester fluids have a higher viscosity, which slightly affects cooling flow. For example, at 40 °C a natural ester might have ~33 cSt viscosity, and even at 100 °C it’s around 8 cSt. This means that in each transformer design, oil flow rate might be lower with ester, and designers often compensate with larger radiators or different core layouts to ensure adequate cooling. Despite the higher viscosity, natural esters have higher specific heat (~2.1 J/g·K) and often slightly better thermal conductivity (~0.17–0.18 W/m·K), which help offset the reduced flow by carrying more heat per unit volume. The net effect is that ester-filled transformers can be designed for similar or even improved cooling performance, though retrofilling a mineral oil transformer with a natural ester might raise its temperatures a bit if not accounted for. Under load stress, natural esters show strong performance: their major advantage is the interaction with insulating paper. Natural esters can retain and dissolve much more water than mineral oil – on the order of 500–600 ppm at typical operating temps (many times the capacity of mineral oil). This means as the paper produces moisture, the ester fluid soaks it up, keeping the paper dry and in better condition. A drier paper maintains its mechanical strength and dielectric integrity much longer. Studies have demonstrated that a transformer retrofilled with natural ester sees the paper’s rate of DP (degree of polymerization) loss slow down, effectively extending insulation life by 20–30% or more, even under higher loading, compared to mineral oil. Additionally, because of the higher allowable hotspot (130 °C vs 110 °C), transformers with natural ester can be purposely designed smaller for a given rating or overloaded in emergencies with less risk. For example, if a transformer is needed to handle occasional peak loads beyond its nominal rating, filling it with natural ester provides extra thermal margin –the fluid and paper can handle the extra heat without immediate damage.Natural esters also have a higher dielectric constant (~3.2 vs 2.2 for mineral oil), which can change the electric field distribution (making it more uniform). This can be beneficial in some insulation structures, potentially raising the partial dischargeinception voltage. Under high electrical stress (like lightning impulses), thereare some differences: ester fluids have a slower impulse dielectric propagation, and at large electrode gaps they may show lower breakdown voltage than mineral oil. But in typical transformer geometries, these differences are minor and managed by design. In summary, under heavy load conditions, natural esters perform very well, provided the transformer is designed or adjusted for their viscosity. They excel at preserving insulation life during thermal overloads, though care must be taken in very cold startups (as natural esters have higher pour points and can thicken in cold weather – e.g. pour point around -20 °C for soybean-based ester, which requires mitigation in cold climates, for bioTRANSOL the same is around -26°C).Synthetic esters under load combine some advantages of both mineral oil and natural ester. Their viscosity is typically between mineral and natural ester. Forinstance, one synthetic ester might be ~28–34 cSt at 40 °C, slightly lowerthan a natural ester, and perhaps ~6 cSt at 100 °C (closer to mineral oil’s 2–3 cSt). Moreover, synthetic esters maintain far better fluidity at low temperatures – manyhave pour points below -50 °C, making them suitable for cold startin sub-zero environments (where natural esters might solidify or require tank heaters).This means in terms of cooling, synthetic esters can handle a wide range of ambient conditions and maintain reasonable viscosity under heavy load heat. Like naturalesters, synthetics also have high moisture saturation limits (often even higher; e.g.>1000 ppm at ambient). They too will pull moisture from cellulose, keeping paper drier under stress. However, some tests indicate natural esters may be slightly better at continuously removing moisture, whereas synthetic esters reach an equilibrium with paper at a higher moisture content. Still, both are vastly superior to mineral oil in this moisture management. Under cyclic or peaky loads, synthetic esters shine because their oxidation stability means the fluid’s properties don’t change much even if the transformer runs hot frequently. There is less concern about rapid acid buildup or fluid deterioration when a synthetic ester unit is pushed hard (for example, in a wind turbine transformer with fluctuating output, or a locomotive transformer with frequent load surges). Additionally, the high thermal stability of synthetic esters can allow for design optimizations – manufacturers have built smaller transformers with synthetic ester that handle the same load as a larger mineral oil unit, by permitting higher operating temperaturessafely. This load capability and compactness were demonstrated in projects like offshore platforms, where using synthetic ester reduced transformer size/weight while meeting power demands. Dielectric performance under load for synthetic esters remains strong; they have dielectric constants similar to natural esters (~3.2) and high breakdown strength. One consideration is that diagnostic methods like Dissolved Gas Analysis (DGA) need recalibration for esters – they generate different gas signatures under faults (e.g. more ethane, less hydrogen/methane compared to mineral oil). But this affects monitoring more than performance.In conclusion, mineral oil offers good baseline performance but with a narrow margin for overloads, while natural and synthetic esters offer enhanced loadtolerance. Natural esters favor scenarios of moderate-to-high loads where longevity and environmental safety are desired, though within sealed systems. Synthetic esters are preferred for extreme or variable load conditions (and cold climates) given theirstability and flexibility. Both ester types improve transformer resilience under stress by keeping insulation drier and allowing higher operating temperature, therebysupporting greater loading without compromising reliability.This makes them very attractive for modern, high-utilization power networks seeking safe and sustainable transformer solutions.References:CIGRÉ WG A2.35, “Experiences in Service with New Insulating Liquids,” Tech.
